The technique

Your face holds
fluid. We move it.

The lymphatic system is a network of vessels and nodes that drains excess fluid and waste from the body's tissues. Unlike the blood system, lymph has no pump — it relies on movement, breathing, and muscular activity to flow. When it stagnates, the evidence shows in your face.

Facial lymphatic drainage uses specific light-touch strokes in a precise directional sequence that follows the lymphatic pathways — from the face toward the major drainage nodes beneath the jaw and in the neck. The technique is gentle but the effect is immediate: the face visibly de-puffs and sharpens as fluid moves where it belongs.

The Nodes

Lymph from the face drains toward nodes in front of the ears, under the jaw, and along the neck. When these pathways are sluggish, fluid backs up. Drainage activates the nodes and restores flow.

The Pressure

Lymphatic vessels lie just beneath the skin — deep pressure pushes past them. The correct technique is light, rhythmic, and specific to the drainage direction. Heavier isn't better here.

The Result

Reduced puffiness, sharper contour, improved skin clarity. The face looks less heavy and more defined. For most clients, the change is visible in the mirror before they leave the studio.

The Timing

Lymphatic drainage is most effective as a course of sessions for chronic congestion, and as a regular maintenance tool for ongoing clarity. A single session before an event delivers immediate visible results.

Your session

What to expect at Soulful Sculpt.

Facial lymphatic drainage is available as a standalone treatment or as part of the full facial sculpting protocol. Sessions run 45–60 minutes. No machines, no devices — manual technique throughout.

01

Assessment & Palpation

Tenielle assesses where fluid is pooling — the under-eye area, cheeks, jawline, and neck — and identifies any areas of particular congestion before beginning the drainage sequence.

02

Neck & Collarbone Opening

Drainage always begins at the terminus — the lymphatic ducts near the collarbone. Opening these before working the face ensures the fluid has somewhere to go. Neck activation precedes facial work.

03

Facial Drainage Sequence

Light, directional strokes follow the lymphatic pathways from the centre of the face outward and downward toward the submandibular and preauricular nodes. The technique is rhythmic, gentle, and precise.

04

Under-Eye & Orbital Focus

The under-eye area requires particular care — the tissue is delicate and the lymphatic vessels extremely superficial. This step addresses puffiness and dark circles related to lymphatic congestion specifically.

05

Sculpting Integration

Where drainage is combined with sculpting strokes, the lymphatic work follows muscle release — allowing the skin to settle into its newly defined position with less fluid holding it in the old shape.

06

Aftercare Guidance

Hydration is essential for 24 hours after lymphatic drainage. Tenielle will advise on session frequency based on your specific presentation — chronic congestion typically responds best to a progressive course.

What you
need to know.

Facial lymphatic drainage is a manual massage technique that uses light, rhythmic strokes to stimulate the lymphatic vessels in the face and neck. Unlike blood, lymph fluid has no pump — it relies on movement and manual massage to flow. When lymph stagnates, the face becomes puffy, dull, and loses definition. Drainage redirects this fluid to the correct nodes, reducing puffiness and sharpening contour.

Regular facial massage uses varied pressure to work the muscles and skin. Lymphatic drainage uses very light, specific pressure in a precise directional sequence that follows the lymphatic pathways — because the lymphatic vessels are just beneath the skin's surface. The technique looks and feels quite different from deep massage, and the mechanism is entirely distinct: it's designed to move fluid, not manipulate muscle.
